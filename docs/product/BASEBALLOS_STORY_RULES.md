# BASEBALLOS_STORY_RULES.md

Version: 1.0

Status: Foundational Product Governance Document

Purpose: Define what qualifies as a BaseballOS story, how stories are selected, what makes a story valuable, and what story patterns should be prioritized or rejected.

This document governs story generation.

It is complementary to:

- BASEBALLOS_WRITING_RULES.md
- BASEBALLOS_NARRATIVE_GOVERNANCE.md

Writing Rules answer:

> How should BaseballOS talk?

Story Rules answer:

> What is worth talking about?

---

# Mission

BaseballOS exists to explain bullpen reality.

Stories should reveal something a baseball fan, analyst, coach, or front office observer may not immediately notice.

BaseballOS stories are not:

- Recaps
- Power rankings
- Team summaries
- Status labels
- Metric descriptions

BaseballOS stories explain:

- What is changing
- Why it is changing
- Why the change matters

---

# Core Principle

Not every bullpen condition deserves a story.

A story exists only when BaseballOS identifies a meaningful bullpen observation.

The presence of data alone is not enough.

---

# The BaseballOS Story Test

Every story must answer all three questions:

### What is happening?

Example:

The same relievers continue absorbing the majority of late-inning workload.

### Why is it happening?

Example:

Recent usage has concentrated heavily around three trusted relievers.

### Why does it matter?

Example:

The manager has fewer paths through a close game than the surface bullpen count suggests.

If a story cannot answer all three questions, it should not exist.

---

# The Hidden Observation Standard

The best BaseballOS stories reveal something that may not be obvious.

The goal is:

> "I hadn't noticed that."

Not:

> "I already knew that."

Good stories surface hidden bullpen realities.

Weak stories restate visible conditions.

---

# Story Selection Hierarchy

When multiple valid observations exist, prioritize stories in this order.

## Tier 1: Highest Value Stories

These should dominate the platform.

### Workload Concentration

Examples:

- Same relievers carrying recent workload
- Workload flowing through a small core
- Narrow leverage group
- Repeated appearance concentration

Reason:

Workload concentration is one of the clearest indicators of bullpen stress before relievers become unavailable.

---

### Late-Inning Path Concentration

Examples:

- Same relievers handling most close games
- Same leverage pocket appearing repeatedly
- Limited trusted late-game options

Reason:

Explains how managers are actually navigating games.

---

### Significant Change Since Yesterday

Examples:

- More rested relievers
- Fewer rested relievers
- New usable options
- Lost flexibility
- Added flexibility

Reason:

BaseballOS should prioritize movement over status.

---

### Usable Depth Narrowing

Examples:

- Surface arm count looks healthy but trusted options are shrinking
- Available bullpen is broader than usable bullpen

Reason:

Fans often miss the difference between roster size and actual game flexibility.

---

### Returning Arm Impact

Examples:

- Returning reliever broadens options
- Returning reliever does not alter leverage structure

Reason:

Adds context beyond simple roster transactions.

---

### Rotation-Driven Bullpen Pressure

Examples:

- Short starts increasing bullpen burden
- Rotation length reducing bullpen burden

Reason:

One of the strongest causes of bullpen condition changes.

---

### Injury-Driven Bullpen Pressure

Examples:

- Missing relievers concentrating workload
- Reduced depth affecting flexibility

Reason:

Provides important context that workload alone cannot explain.

---

# Tier 2: Valuable Supporting Stories

These are useful but should not dominate.

Examples:

- Defined first bullpen group
- Multiple paths through late innings
- Leverage center remains unchanged
- Stable bullpen structure
- Broad workload distribution

---

# Tier 3: Fallback Stories

Only surface when stronger observations do not exist.

Examples:

- General availability movement
- Mild workload changes
- Minor bullpen structure adjustments

---

# Story Rejection Rules

The following are not stories.

Reject them.

---

### Generic Status Statements

Bad:

- Bullpen is under pressure
- Bullpen has limited flexibility
- Bullpen is manageable
- Bullpen is stable

These are conditions.

Not observations.

---

### Metric Restatements

Bad:

- Stress increased
- Coverage declined
- Availability changed

These are outputs.

Not stories.

---

### Obvious Statements

Bad:

- Team has available relievers
- Team has relievers on watch
- Team has relievers needing rest

These describe the dashboard.

They do not explain the dashboard.

---

### Empty Shape Descriptions

Bad:

- Current relief shape is easy to see
- Bullpen shape changed
- Relief shape starts here

Shape without meaning is not a story.

---

### Roster Summaries

Bad:

- Team has nine relievers
- Team activated a reliever

Unless the event changes bullpen reality, it is not a story.

---

# Story Quality Requirements

Every story should contain:

## Observation

What BaseballOS noticed.

## Explanation

Why it is happening.

## Consequence

Why users should care.

All three are required.

---

# Managerial Consequence Requirement

Every story should include a baseball consequence.

Examples:

Good:

The bullpen has fewer trusted paths through a close game.

Good:

The manager may need to lean on the same leverage group again.

Good:

The bullpen has more flexibility than it did earlier this week.

Bad:

The bullpen changed.

Bad:

The bullpen is different.

Bad:

The bullpen has a new shape.

---

# Story Diversity Rules

BaseballOS should avoid repeating the same story archetype excessively.

If ten teams qualify for the same observation type:

Do not surface all ten.

Surface the strongest examples.

Promote alternative valid stories elsewhere.

---

# Story Archetype Diversity Targets

BaseballOS should maintain healthy variety across:

- Workload concentration
- Rested-option movement
- Returning-arm impact
- Late-inning route concentration
- Multiple-path flexibility
- Rotation-created pressure
- Injury-created pressure
- Narrowing usable depth
- Expanding usable depth
- Leverage-center stability
- Leverage-center change

The feed should feel varied.

Not repetitive.

---

# Story Priority Tie-Breakers

When multiple stories qualify:

Prioritize:

1. Biggest recent change
2. Strongest managerial consequence
3. Most hidden observation
4. Strongest bullpen impact
5. Strongest supporting evidence

Avoid arbitrary selection.

---

# Story Evidence Requirements

Stories should be supported by visible bullpen realities.

Examples:

Good:

Recent workload concentrated among three relievers.

Good:

Rested-option count increased from two to six.

Good:

Returning reliever added depth without changing leverage structure.

Avoid unsupported claims.

---

# Story Headline Standards

Headlines should communicate the observation itself.

Good:

The Kansas City Royals bullpen work is flowing through a small core.

Good:

The New York Mets still have multiple ways to cover a close game.

Good:

The bullpen looks rested, but the same relievers keep carrying the leverage work.

Bad:

Bullpen Status Update.

Bad:

Workload Situation.

Bad:

Current Relief Shape.

Bad:

Bullpen Overview.

---

# Story Curiosity Standard

A user should want to read the explanation after seeing the headline.

Good:

The bullpen looks rested, but the same relievers keep carrying the leverage work.

This creates curiosity.

Bad:

Six relievers are available.

This does not.

---

# Story Freshness Rules

Prefer:

- New developments
- Emerging trends
- Changes since yesterday
- Changes since last week

Avoid:

- Static descriptions that repeat daily

Movement is generally more valuable than status.

---

# Story Integrity Rules

Never:

- Predict outcomes
- Predict wins or losses
- Recommend betting decisions
- Rank teams by quality
- Invent reasons not supported by data
- Imply injuries without roster evidence
- Imply live-game context from completed-game data

Stories explain.

Stories do not forecast.

---

# Final Story Validation

Before surfacing a story, ask:

1. Is this an observation or merely a label?
2. Would a baseball fan learn something new?
3. Does the story explain why the observation matters?
4. Does the story have a managerial consequence?
5. Is the observation specific?
6. Is the observation supported by available data?
7. Is this one of the strongest available stories today?

If any answer is "No," the story should be reconsidered.

---

# What BaseballOS Is Trying To Own

BaseballOS is not trying to own:

- Predictions
- Betting
- Rankings
- Team power scores

BaseballOS is trying to own:

"How bullpen conditions are changing, why they are changing, and why those changes matter."

Every story should move users closer to understanding that question.
